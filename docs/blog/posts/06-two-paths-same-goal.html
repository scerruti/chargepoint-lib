Nikki at Transport Evolved retrofitted a JuiceBox with OpenEVSE hardware and Node-RED to enable load-sharing and smarter scheduling. I extended a CPH50 with a serverless script. Different hardware, different skills, same goal: make home charging smarter.

Context Differences

Nikki's project
320A service, multiple EVSEs, hardware retrofit, load-sharing focus.
My project
100A service, single CPH50, no hardware changes, schedule extension focus.

Skill Profiles

  • Nikki: Hardware generalist, comfortable opening the EVSE, swapping boards, wiring CT clamps, using Node-RED for flows.
  • Me: Software-first, TypeScript/AI workflows, zero hardware changes, cloud automation.

Approach Trade-offs

  • Hardware retrofit: Gains deep control (pilot signal, per-phase monitoring), but requires safety diligence and physical modifications.
  • API-layer automation: Non-invasive, leverages existing charger capabilities, but depends on reverse-engineered endpoints and uptime of the vendor APIs.

Common Ground

  • Both lean on community knowledge‚ÄîOpenEVSE docs for Nikki; python-chargepoint and forum threads for me.
  • Both preserve something valuable: Nikki keeps her hardware investment; I keep Energy Price Plan scheduling.
  • Both add a safety valve: manual override still works (Charge Now in the app; physical controls on the EVSE).
There is no single ‚Äúright‚Äù way to extend a charger. Your constraints‚Äîservice size, budget, trust model, and skills‚Äîdecide the path.

Why This Comparison Matters

It's tempting to claim one approach is superior. The reality: they're optimized for different constraints. Watching Nikki's build validated that DIY energy projects are thriving across hardware and software. It also reinforced my design choice: zero hardware, keep the vendor scheduling, add one precise automation.
